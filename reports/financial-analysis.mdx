The Financial Analysis report is the most complete picture of your church's financial health that Altarflow produces. It pulls together everything flowing into and out of your church — giving, operating expenses, fund activity — and presents it in one view designed for pastors and administrators, not accountants. You do not need a finance background to understand it or to use it confidently in a leadership meeting.

## What the Financial Analysis report shows

The report is built around six key areas:

**Income vs. expenses** is the foundational view. Two bars or lines side by side for each period you select, showing total money coming in and total money going out. At a glance you can see whether your church is operating at a surplus or a deficit for any given stretch of time.

**Burn rate** tells you how fast your church is spending its available funds. If your church holds three months of operating reserves and your current burn rate is $18,000 per month, you can see precisely how long those reserves last. This number is especially useful when cash flow is seasonal — like summer months when giving typically dips.

**Net income growth** tracks whether your church's financial position is improving over time. A rising net income line means more is coming in than going out, and that trend is strengthening. A flat or falling line is an early signal to review spending or re-engage your donor base.

**Donation trends** shows giving month-over-month and year-over-year. You can see whether the congregation's generosity is growing, stable, or declining — and whether any particular period (a capital campaign, a holiday season, a community event) produced a notable spike.

**Operating costs** breaks down your expense side into categories so you can see where the church's money actually goes: facilities, personnel, ministry programs, outreach, administration. This makes it easy to spot if one area is growing disproportionately.

## Reading the report as a non-accountant

You do not need to know debits from credits to get value from this report. Here is how to read each section without an accounting background:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Start with the income vs. expenses comparison">
    Look at the two lines or bars for your selected period. If the income bar is taller than the expense bar, your church took in more than it spent — that is a surplus. If expenses are higher, you spent more than you received.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check the burn rate">
    The burn rate number tells you your average monthly spend. Compare it to your current account balance to understand how many months of runway your church has. A common target is three to six months of operating expenses held in reserve.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Look at the trend lines">
    The donation trend chart shows whether giving is moving up or down over time. Month-to-month swings are normal. The direction over six to twelve months is what matters.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review operating costs by category">
    Scroll to the expense breakdown. If one category is consuming an unusually large share of your budget — or growing much faster than others — that is worth a conversation with your finance team or board.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Filtering by date range or fund

By default the Financial Analysis report covers the current calendar year. You can adjust this from the filter panel:

- **Date range** — switch to a custom range, a specific quarter, or a prior year to compare periods side by side
- **Fund** — filter the entire report to a single fund (General Fund, Building Fund, Missions Fund, etc.) to see its income, expenses, and burn rate in isolation
- **Multiple funds** — select two or more funds to compare their financial performance in the same view

Filtering by fund is particularly useful when your church runs restricted funds. Board members or committee chairs can see exactly how their fund is performing without seeing unrelated financial data.

## Using the Financial Analysis report in leadership meetings

<Tip>
  When you share the Financial Analysis report with your leadership team, you have two options: share a **live link** that updates automatically as new transactions come in, or **export a PDF snapshot** that captures the numbers as of the moment you generated it. Use the live link for ongoing visibility between meetings and the PDF snapshot for the official board packet — that way the minutes reflect exactly what the board reviewed, not a figure that has since changed.
</Tip>

Here is how to make the most of the Financial Analysis report in a board or elder meeting:

1. **Open with the income vs. expenses summary.** It sets the context for everything else and answers the question every board member is thinking: "Are we in good shape?"
2. **Show the donation trend.** If giving is growing, celebrate it. If it is flat or declining, name it early so the conversation stays constructive.
3. **Walk through operating costs by category.** This is where most of the board's questions will land. Having the breakdown ready prevents guesswork.
4. **End with the burn rate.** Put your reserves in context. If you are building toward a major expense (a new facility, a ministry launch), show how the burn rate affects your timeline.

The report is available in English and Spanish. If your board includes Spanish-speaking members, toggle the language before opening the meeting so everyone is reading the same numbers in their preferred language.
