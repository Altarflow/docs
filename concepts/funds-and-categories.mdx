Fund accounting is the financial framework churches and nonprofits use to track money by its designated purpose — not just by income and expense. Altarflow builds fund accounting into every layer of the platform, so every donation and expense is automatically tagged to the fund it belongs to. The result is a real-time picture of what each fund holds, what's been spent, and what's available — without running a custom report or opening a spreadsheet.

## What a fund is

A fund in Altarflow represents a pool of money set aside for a specific purpose. Funds come in two types:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Unrestricted Funds" icon="unlock">
    Money the church can use for any operating purpose. The **General Fund** is the most common example — tithes and general offerings that cover staff, utilities, and day-to-day ministry operations.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Restricted Funds" icon="lock">
    Money given for a specific designated purpose. A donor who gives to the **Building Fund** or **Missions Fund** has restricted that gift — it cannot be used for operating expenses without their consent.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

This distinction matters legally and ethically. When a donor designates a gift to a specific fund, your church has an obligation to spend it only for that purpose. Altarflow tracks this automatically so you never accidentally spend restricted dollars on general operations.

## Common fund examples

Most churches start with three to five funds and add more as ministries grow:

| Fund Name | Type | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|
| General Fund | Unrestricted | Staff salaries, utilities, supplies, rent |
| Building Fund | Restricted | Construction, renovation, facilities |
| Missions Fund | Restricted | Local outreach, international partnerships |
| Youth Ministry | Restricted or Unrestricted | Events, curriculum, retreats |
| Emergency Fund | Unrestricted | Unexpected repairs or operational gaps |

<Tip>
  Start simple. Create your General Fund first and add restricted funds only when you have active campaigns or designated giving for a specific purpose. You can add funds at any time and backfill transactions.
</Tip>

## How categories work

While funds answer *what purpose* money belongs to, categories answer *what type* of transaction it is. Categories are used primarily for expenses and appear on financial reports so your board can see spending patterns at a glance.

Examples of expense categories:
- **Facilities** — rent, utilities, cleaning
- **Personnel** — salaries, contractor payments, honoraria
- **Ministry** — curriculum, supplies, event costs
- **Technology** — software, equipment, streaming
- **Fuel** — pastoral travel, vehicle expenses

Every expense in Altarflow carries both a **fund** (which pool of money paid for it) and a **category** (what kind of expense it was). This two-axis tagging is what makes Altarflow's reports useful for both board oversight and year-end compliance.

## How every transaction gets tagged

Altarflow tags transactions to funds and categories through three pathways:

<Steps>
  <Step title="AI agent categorization">
    When a transaction hits your connected bank feed, the AI agent assigns a suggested fund and category based on the vendor, amount, and transaction type. For recurring vendors like your electricity provider, the agent learns your preference after one correction and applies it automatically every time.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Digital giving fund selection">
    When a donor gives through Quick Give or your web portal, they choose a fund at the time of giving. The donation is tagged to that fund the moment it processes — no manual reconciliation needed.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Manual entry and override">
    Any transaction can be reassigned to a different fund or category at any time. Manual donations (cash, check, envelope) are entered with a fund and category in the donation entry form. You always have the final word on how transactions are tagged.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Real-time fund balances

Once your transactions are tagged, Altarflow maintains a live balance for each fund. Navigate to **Reports → Fund Balances** to see:

- **Total received** — all donations designated to this fund
- **Total spent** — all expenses charged against this fund
- **Current balance** — what remains available

<Warning>
  A negative fund balance means you've spent more from a restricted fund than has been received — a compliance risk. Altarflow displays a warning banner when any fund goes negative so you can investigate immediately.
</Warning>

## Why this matters for your church

Fund accounting is not just an accounting preference — it shapes the trust your board, donors, and denomination place in your ministry:

**Board reporting.** When your board asks how much is left in the Building Fund or whether the Missions Fund is on track, Altarflow generates that answer in seconds. Board members see clean, fund-level reports without needing access to your full books.

**Donor trust.** A donor who designated $5,000 to the Building Fund can see exactly how much of their gift remains in that fund via the donor portal. Transparency at that level turns one-time givers into long-term partners.

**Compliance.** Nonprofits and churches with denominational oversight are often required to demonstrate that restricted funds were used appropriately. Altarflow's fund-level transaction history is audit-ready at any time.

**Campaign clarity.** When you run a fundraising campaign — a new LED screen, a mission trip, a facility renovation — you can tie a fund directly to that campaign and show real-time progress to your congregation.

## Setting up your funds

You can create and manage funds from **Settings → Funds**. Each fund has a name, a type (restricted or unrestricted), an optional description, and an optional spending limit. Changes take effect immediately across all reports and the agent categorization queue.

<Tip>
  If you're migrating from another platform, Altarflow's migration team can map your existing fund structure and import historical transactions with the correct fund tags preserved.
</Tip>
